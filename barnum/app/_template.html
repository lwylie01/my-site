Why this works, and why it matters

This is the Barnum effect (or Forer effect): people rate vague, generic descriptions as highly accurate when they believe the descriptions were made just for them. In 1949 the psychologist Bertram Forer gave his students a personality "test," then handed every single one of them the same profile. They rated it 4.26 out of 5 for accuracy.

This is a lesson about measurement, not horoscopes.

A profile that fits everyone equally well tells you about no one. A measurement that can't tell people apart isn't measuring anything. That's construct validity: the question of whether a tool captures the real thing it claims to. The next time a dashboard, an engagement score, or a "data-driven" personality assessment feels strikingly accurate, ask the Barnum question: would it feel just as true if it were about someone else?
